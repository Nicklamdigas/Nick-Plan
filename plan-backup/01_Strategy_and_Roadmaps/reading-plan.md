# Nick's Strategic Reading Plan

## 🎯 The Goal
To support the transition from "AI Prompter" to independent Software Engineer, focusing on focus, mindset, clean code, and eventual system design mastery.

---

## 📚 Recommended Reading Order (The "April Sprint" List)

### 1. **Atomic Habits** by James Clear
*   **When to read:** Starting tonight (20:30 slot)
*   **Why:** You are building a massive new routine. This book is the manual for making it stick. It will help you stay consistent even on days when your motivation is low.

### 2. **Deep Work** by Cal Newport
*   **When to read:** Immediately after (or alongside) Atomic Habits.
*   **Why:** Teaches you how to execute the 2-hour "Logic" and "Implementation" blocks in your schedule without checking your phone or getting distracted.

### 3. **Python Crash Course** (3rd Edition) by Eric Matthes
*   **When to read:** Phase 1 (Core Study)
*   **Why:** This is your primary technical textbook. It is better than `IntroToPython.pdf` because it is project-based. Use the "Project" sections in the second half of the book to guide your own builds.

### 4. **Grokking Algorithms** (2nd Edition) by Aditya Bhargava
*   **When to read:** Phase 1 & 2 (Logic Phase)
*   **Why:** Best visual intro to Big O, sorting, and recursion. It makes the "Logic" part of your schedule much easier to understand than traditional academic books.

### 5. **The Pragmatic Programmer** (20th Anniversary Edition) by Hunt & Thomas
*   **When to read:** Phase 1 & 2 (Mindset slot)
*   **Why:** Transition from "writing code" to "Software Engineering." This book is specifically for your goal of becoming an independent developer who doesn't rely on magic.

### 6. **Clean Code** by Robert C. Martin
*   **When to read:** Phase 2 (Project Build block)
*   **Why:** Teaches you how to write professional, readable code for your portfolio. Essential before you start the School Management System.

### 7. **Designing Data-Intensive Applications (DDIA)** by Martin Kleppmann
*   **When to read:** Phase 4 (Distributed Computing Unit)
*   **Why:** Senior-level architecture. Read this when you're ready to understand how large-scale systems (like Facebook or Amazon) handle data.

### 8. **Academic References** (Ian Sommerville / Rajib Mall)
*   **When to read:** May onwards (Zetech Term)
*   **Why:** Use these to dominate your exams. Match the chapters to your course syllabus.

## 🛠️ Reading Strategy (How to actually learn)

Since you're new to technical reading, use this **Hybrid Approach**:

### 1. Mindset/Non-Technical Books (*Atomic Habits, Deep Work*)
*   **Strategy:** Read like a normal book.
*   **Action:** Write down **one** thing you will change in your routine tomorrow.

### 2. Narrative Technical Books (*The Pragmatic Programmer, Clean Code*)
*   **Strategy:** Read one chapter at a time.
*   **Action:** Ask: "How could I have applied this to my code today?"

### 3. Hands-on Technical Books (*Python Crash Course, Grokking Algorithms*)
*   **Strategy:** The "Read then Build" (Gold Standard).
*   **Action:** Read a chapter or concept until you understand the "Why." Then, close the book, open a blank file in VS Code, and try to build a small tool that uses that concept. 
*   **Example:** If you read about "List Comprehensions," try to rewrite your `todo.py` filtering logic from memory. If you get stuck, look back at the book briefly, then close it again. This builds "Muscle Memory," not "Copy Memory."

### 4. Manuals/Reference Books (*Ian Sommerville*)
*   **Strategy:** The "Search & Destroy" method.
*   **Action:** Only read the sections required for your Zetech assignments.

## 📅 The 30-Minute Routine (20:30 - 21:00)
1. **No Phone:** Put your phone in another room.
2. **Read:** 20 minutes of focused reading.
3. **Reflect:** 10 minutes thinking about how it applies to your Zetech studies or your Python projects.
4. **Commit:** If you had a "lightbulb moment," add it to your `dev-journal` entry for the day.
